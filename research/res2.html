The Relationship between Adaptation Disorders and Victimization

2024 Dongguk University

Overview

This study investigates the relationship between adaptation disorders and crime victimization among North Korean defectors using a quantitative statistical framework. Grounded in labeling theory, the research models how rigid gender norms and maladaptive perceptions influence vulnerability to crime during social integration.

Data & Variables

  • Dataset: Korean Institute of Criminology survey (n ≈ 210)
  • Independent Variable: Sexual adaptation disorder (Likert-scale composite)
  • Dependent Variable: Crime victimization (binary outcome)
  • Tools: R, SPSS 25

Statistical Approach

  • Descriptive Analysis: Mean, median, and mode across variables (~3.0), indicating consistent gender norm perceptions
  • Normality Testing: All variables satisfied skewness (<3) and kurtosis (<20), validating parametric assumptions
  • Reliability Analysis: Cronbach’s α = 0.444 → relatively low internal consistency
  • Validity Testing: KMO = 0.581, Bartlett’s test p = 0.01 → acceptable construct validity
  • Correlation Analysis: No statistically significant relationships (p > 0.05 across all variables)
  • Binary Logistic Regression: Used to model probability of victimization

Key Results

  • Logistic regression showed a positive but non-significant relationship between adaptation disorder and victimization
  • Odds Ratio: 1.091 → each unit increase slightly increases risk
  • p-value = 0.779 → not statistically significant (95% CI not satisfied)
  • Model explanatory power extremely low (Nagelkerke R² ≈ 0.001)
  • Classification accuracy: 76.4%

Interpretation

Although statistical significance was not achieved, the model reveals a directional trend suggesting that maladaptive gender perceptions may increase vulnerability to crime. The findings highlight structural and psychological risk factors that are not easily captured through traditional statistical thresholds, emphasizing the need for data-informed policy interventions.

Impact

  • Proposed data-driven policy recommendations for vulnerable populations
  • Designed intervention strategies including education and mentorship systems
  • Demonstrated application of statistical modeling to real-world social issues
